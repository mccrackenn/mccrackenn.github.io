  1. Grain/Malted Barley

    2. Barley, grain in general, makes the biggest contribution to beer. Barely gives the beer it's color, underlying flavor, sweetness, and body. Malting is the process of wetting barley kernels and allowing them to sprout. After the malting process, the barley is now known as malt or barley malt.

  2. Hops

    Hops are pinecone-like flowers that are harvested on enormous trellises. They are in the same family as cannabis. Hops are used for multiple reasons in beer brewing. First they contain acids that act as a bittering agent to counteract the sweet malted barely. These acids also act a preservative that inhibits bacteria growth and keeps beer longer. Lastly hops provide aroma to a beer. Many different types of hops are grown and they each have their own quality/purpose. A beer's bitterness is measured in International Bitterness Units (IBUs) and many brewers have IBUs printed on their labels.

  3. Yeast

    Yeast is what drives the fermentation process. Yeast is a simple singe cell in the fungus family that is self-reproducing. Yeast has a huge appetite for sweet liquids and produces tremendous quantities of alcohol and carbon dioxide while feasting on beer wort. Beer wort is the incredibly sweet syrupy liquid that is produced from the malted grains being soaked/boiled in hot water. Hundreds of different types of yeast strains are in use and produce an array of flavors. Yeast determines the classification of a beer. Brewmasters pick a yeast according to the recipe or style of beer they want to make. Yeast is identified as either an ale yeast or a lager yeast. Ale yeast is a top-fermenting strain that functions efficiently in temperatures of 60 to 75 degrees. Lager yeast is a bottom-fermenting strain that functions best in cooler temps of 38 to 52 degrees.

  4. Water

    Water-The quality of water used in brewing cannot be understated because water makes up ninety-five percent of beer. Many brewers, such as the Coors Brewing, frequently advertise the high quality of the Rocky Mountain water used for their beer. Without taking this conversation too far down the rabbit hole into hydrology and chemistry, a basic principle exists that if one’s tap water source is good enough to drink, it’s good enough to use for brewing. The tap water in most areas of the Northwest is of high quality and has not been a concern for my brews, however other regions have what is known as “hard water”. Hard water usually means the water is high in the amount of calcium, magnesium ions and other minerals. These minerals can negatively affect the beer. One can purchase a test kit to determine the amount of minerals in their water and make adjustments via adding agents or other methods.
